a data story in eleven eras

are the eras actually real?

Everyone talks about Taylor Swift's "eras" like they're different artists. I put that to the test: 582 songs spanning 2006 to 2024, nine Spotify audio features, PCA and K-means clustering. The result is a map of 18 years of her music, debut through TTPD, and yes, the eras show up.
Just not the way you'd expect.

how the sound shifted

Each bar is a release year, split by how much of that year's music belongs to each sound. Pop dominates the early and middle career; from 2020 the acoustic singer-songwriter sound (green) takes over the top spot, with 2021 and 2023 mixing in the re-recorded pop catalog.

what it means

✳

the eras are real, but blurry

Stylistic change is gradual, with overlapping clusters rather than abrupt shifts. Every era contains multiple sounds; Taylor evolves by shifting the mix, not by flipping a switch.

✳

2020 is the hinge

Pop clusters dominate nearly every year from 2006 to 2019. From folklore onward, the acoustic singer-songwriter sound takes the top spot in original releases, and only the re-recordings pull the old pop sound back into the charts.

✳

outliers are on purpose

The tiny experimental cluster (Vigilante Shit, the 1989 voice memos) isn't statistical noise. It's deliberate experimentation, the sketchbook pages of the catalog.

✳

why marketers should care

Clusters, not albums, are the natural playlists. Mood-based sets, catalog marketing and audience targeting all work better when songs are grouped by how they sound rather than when they dropped.

Method: 582 tracks, 9 standardized Spotify audio features, PCA (3 components, 59% of variance), K-means on all 3 components (k=4, chosen by elbow method). Era colors group album editions for display only; the model never sees album or date information. Python, pandas, scikit-learn.
